communication

five simple rules

a soft skills initiative

  • Intro to communication, stolen from AIT
  • Series of simple rules
  • Mostly about customer communications, but transferable
  • Part of a new soft skills initiative!
#1

communicate more

  • Amazing bang for buck
  • No matter how great you are, if you don't communicate, your customer won't know
  • Andy T's package delivery example:
    1. No comms:
      • Package fails to arrive Tues when you want.
      • Arrives Fri instead - 3 days late!
      • You vow to never use that delivery company again.
    2. Good comms:
      • They ring you on Monday, and say it's out of stock, so will be delayed - will update on Weds
      • On Weds, they ring and say they've moved heaven & earth to find a supplier - will be with you on Fri!
      • Package arrives on Friday as promised
      • You're impressed by their great service
  • Only difference is a couple of quick calls
  • If it hurts, do it more: if you're scared of communicating, DO IT MORE. Bad news only festers.
#2

no surprises

  • You hate the package delivery company because they surprised you
  • Customers hate suprises too
    • Overspend (even if justified)
    • Late releases
    • Holiday / reduced support
  • To avoid surprises, you need to guarantee they know what you think they do
  • That means being EXPLICIT, not assuming they can work it out
  • Example: A tight support budget + lots of small tasks which we tell them the individual cost = surprise overspend
  • Solution: Show the total spent, include projections, and explicitly tell them they're going to go over
#3

email less

  • "Choose your medium carefully"
  • Email's good for everyday info & recording decisions
  • Rubbish for big discussions, negotiations, scary discussions, or getting prompt info
  • Might think email is better if you're nervous - actually, you're making it harder, as you don't get to react
  • If you're not sure what someone's reaction will be, ALWAYS talk on phone / in person
  • You can judge their reaction and tailor the message, and if they're angry they get to vent a bit (seriously!)
#4

keep it simple

  • Lots of text turns people off - keep to the core message
  • That means you need to KNOW THE CORE MESSAGE - make sure it's specific. What should they know afterwards?
  • Repeating snippets in recurring mails also turns people off - at the very least, rephrase (or chop it out!)
  • A picture is worth a thousand words, and keeps people interested - burn-up charts > description

assume good faith

#5
  • Not about the style of communication, but about a mindset it's helpful to be in
  • Easy to say "Customer is so stupid they ..." / "Can't believe they've asked for ..."
  • Assume your audience is rational. If they're frustrating, then you don't understand: communicate more!
  • Common to complain that they don't get back to us - but we send huge emails full of hard questions which they have to fit in around their day job, so no wonder they don't feel like making a start on it
  • Maybe a meeting would be better? Or a (video?) call? We might get answers faster, and influence them to be the 'right' ones
  • Even if you can't believe it, acting as if they're reasonable will keep you calm, and help you be clearer and make better choices

summary

  • communicate more
  • avoid surprises (by communicating more)
  • email less (but communicate more)
  • keep it simple
  • assume good faith
